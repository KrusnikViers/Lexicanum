# {{n-g-lite|Connects at least two alternative [[word]]s, [[phrase]]s, [[clause]]s, [[sentences]], [[etc.]], each of which could make a passage [[true]].}}

# {{n-g-lite|Counts the [[elements]] [[before]] and [[after]] as two [[possibilities]].}}
# [[otherwise|Otherwise]] {{gloss|a consequence of the condition that the previous is false}}.
#: {{ux-lite|en|It's raining! Come inside '''or''' you'll catch a cold!}}

=====Usage notes=====
* {{s|connecting alternative terms}} When not implied by the meaning of the [[conjoin#Noun|conjoin]]s, it is generally ambiguous whether “or” is intended in an exclusive or inclusive sense. In speech, various means may be used to convey exclusivity, such as stress on the word “or” or a rising intonation before it.<ref>{{cite-book|en|last=Huddleston|first=Rodney|year=1988|title=English Grammar: An Outline|publisher=Cambridge University Press|isbn=0521311527|pages=198–99}}</ref> In a formal or technical register, {{m|en|and/or}} may be used to specify inclusivity.
